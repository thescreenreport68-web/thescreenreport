There have been many Batmen, but few have been allowed to simply be a detective. Matt Reeves' **The Batman** (2022) corrects that, and **this The Batman review comes down firmly on the side of the film**: it is a confident, rain-drenched reinvention and the most genuinely noir entry the character has had on the big screen. If you want a popcorn superhero romp, look elsewhere. If you want a serial-killer thriller wearing a cowl, this is it, and it is one of the more accomplished comic-book films in years.

The verdict up front: this is a roughly three-hour crime story that treats Gotham as a sickness and Bruce Wayne as a man still figuring out whether he is the cure or a symptom. It is moody, methodical, and unafraid of silence. Reeves and star Robert Pattinson make a deliberate bet early on, that audiences will follow Batman not because he punches well but because he wants to know who did it. That bet mostly pays off, and when it does, the film feels like something genuinely new for the franchise.

## What The Batman Is About

Set in the vigilante's second year on the streets, the story finds a younger, rawer Bruce Wayne who has not yet become the polished billionaire-philanthropist of legend. He is a recluse, a creature of the night who narrates his nocturnal patrols with grim self-seriousness. When a masked killer known as the Riddler (Paul Dano) begins murdering Gotham's powerful figures and leaving puzzles addressed directly to Batman, the case drags our hero into the rot beneath the city's institutions.

What follows is structured less like a typical comic-book movie and more like a procedural. Batman works the crime scenes alongside Lieutenant Jim Gordon (Jeffrey Wright), interrogates suspects, follows the money, and slowly realizes the conspiracy reaches into his own family's history. Crucially, the central premise is investigation, not escalation. The threat is a riddle to be solved, and the film trusts you to want to solve it too. I will keep the specifics light here, because much of the pleasure is in watching the pieces click.

## Direction and Craft: Gotham as Character

Reeves directs with enormous control, and the craft is the film's headline achievement. Gotham has rarely felt this lived-in and this oppressive. It is perpetually wet, lit in sodium-orange and deep shadow, a city that seems to be drowning before our eyes. Cinematographer Greig Fraser shoots much of it in tight, grimy close quarters that make even the action feel claustrophobic rather than liberating.

A mid-film car chase involving the Batmobile is a brutal, low-slung piece of staging, all roaring engine and headlight glare, that ranks among the best the franchise has produced. And Michael Giacchino's score is a genuine character of its own, anchored by a heavy, marching theme that lends the whole picture a funereal weight. Reeves leans on horror grammar more than spectacle grammar here, framing Batman's first appearances as something to be feared. That "fear" of the Bat is treated as a real, tactical thing, and the choice gives the early stretches a charge that more conventional hero entrances lack.

If you appreciated the grounded, street-level approach of the [Christopher Nolan Batman trilogy](/reviews/the-dark-knight-review), you will recognize the DNA, though Reeves pushes further into mood and further from kinetic action.

## Performances: Pattinson Plays the Wound

Robert Pattinson is the right kind of strange for this Batman. He plays Bruce as a haunted, withdrawn man whose pain has not yet calcified into the controlled mask of older interpretations. His Batman is more convincing than his Bruce Wayne, which is by design here, and Pattinson commits fully to the brooding, almost vampiric stillness the role demands. It is an internal performance, and it works because he never begs for sympathy.

The supporting cast is uniformly strong. Zoe Kravitz brings real heat and ambivalence to Selina Kyle, and her scenes with Pattinson generate the film's only genuine warmth. Jeffrey Wright gives Gordon a weary decency that grounds the partnership. Colin Farrell, buried under prosthetics as the Penguin, is a scene-stealer whose work here later led to a spin-off series. And Paul Dano commits to the Riddler with an unhinged intensity that tips, in places, into something disturbing and pathetic at once, a far cry from campier versions of the character. Fans drawn to that villain might also enjoy our take on [Joker](/reviews/joker-review), another study of a broken man and a broken city.

## What Works

The film's greatest strength is its discipline. It commits to being a detective story and refuses to bail itself out with constant set pieces. The atmosphere is total. The sense of place, the score, the photography, and the deliberate pacing combine into an experience that feels authored rather than assembled by committee. When the central mystery is humming, the film is gripping in a way superhero movies rarely manage.

It also takes its themes seriously. The script is genuinely interested in cycles of violence, in inherited corruption, and in whether vengeance can ever actually fix anything. Pattinson's arc, from an avatar of pure retribution toward something closer to hope, gives the long runtime a real emotional spine. For viewers tired of the weightless, quippy register of so many comic adaptations, the seriousness lands as a relief.

## What Falls Short

The runtime is the obvious cost. At roughly two hours and 55 minutes, The Batman asks for patience, and the middle stretch sags once the novelty of the world settles in. A leaner cut might have lost nothing essential. The film's relentless grimness, while consistent, can also become monotonous; there is almost no levity to break the gloom, and a few more tonal grace notes would have helped.

The plotting, for all its detective trappings, occasionally relies on convenient leaps and a villain whose master plan strains credibility once fully revealed. And while the climax pivots toward genuine large-scale stakes, that shift sits slightly awkwardly against the intimate noir that precedes it, as if the movie briefly remembers it is obligated to be a blockbuster. These are real flaws, not fatal ones, but they keep an excellent film from being a flawless one. ## The Verdict

The Batman is a triumph of mood and intent, the rare modern superhero film that knows exactly what it wants to be and mostly gets there. Reeves delivers a Gotham you can feel in your bones, Pattinson delivers a Bruce Wayne defined by his wounds, and the whole production prioritizes character and atmosphere over spectacle. Its length and unbroken bleakness will test some viewers, and the plot is not airtight under scrutiny. But as a piece of filmmaking, it is assured, distinctive, and genuinely thrilling when the mystery is in motion.

## Should You Watch The Batman?

This is a Batman built for people who want to sit with the character rather than be dazzled by him. On that promise, it delivers. Recommended, with the caveat that you should be in the mood for a long night in the rain.
