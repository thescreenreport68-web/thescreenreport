The new film *Borderline* has arrived, and the internet did what it does best: it split right down the middle. While one performance is earning raves, the rest of the film is getting dragged through the mud with accusations of being a politically funded message piece. The reaction was instant — and loud.

## The One Performance Fans Are Raving About

In a sea of negative takes, one element consistently stands out as a bright spot. Critic Robert Evans summed it up: "Every scene with Forest in this movie is one of the most electrifying things I've ever seen. The rest of the movie is borderline unwatchable."

This sentiment resonated with some viewers online. One user echoed the technical challenges and specific nature of the film: "Dialogue movie. Has very less action. Need subtitles for Spanish parts." It suggests an experience that requires patience, but for many, that patience wasn't rewarded by the story itself. The praise for actor Forest's work becomes a lonely island in a broader reaction defined by skepticism.

## The Propaganda Accusation That Stole the Show

For a vocal segment of the audience, the discussion isn't about cinematic technique but about perceived political messaging. The most common criticism accuses *Borderline* of being a funded message piece. One viewer posted bluntly: "Nice propaganda to discredit the current presidents efforts to rid the country of illegal aliens."

This interpretation wasn't isolated. Another user, while offering a slightly more measured observation, landed in a similar place of suspicion: "I watched it. Lots going on. I’d love to know about the background of who funded it. Seems different. Am thinking it’s a message funded by us gov." This focus on funding and intent has come to define the public conversation, often overshadowing any discussion of plot, character, or theme. The reaction has curdled into a political litmus test, with many dismissing the film outright based on perceived allegiances rather than artistic merits.

## How a Divided Audience Got Here

The *Borderline* controversy highlights how audience reception can fracture along completely different fault lines. For some, it's a great performance trapped in a mediocre film. For others, the very premise is viewed through a lens of deep political distrust. The film's dialogue-heavy, subtitled format may have further alienated viewers, making it easier for political interpretations to dominate. In an era of heightened polarization, a film touching on themes like immigration was almost guaranteed to become a political football.

## The Discourse Isn't Cooling Off Any Time Soon

The conversation around *Borderline* seems firmly stuck. It's less likely to evolve into a nuanced debate about craft and more likely to remain a binary shouting match between those who see propaganda and those who see a failed drama with one brilliant performance. The standout praise for Forest may become a footnote, remembered primarily as the sole positive in a project overwhelmed by political controversy.
