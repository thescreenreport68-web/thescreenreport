Apple TV+’s freshman comedy *The Studio* enters the 2026 Emmy race not just as a defending champion, but as a historic record-holder that has reset the expectations for a first-year series.

Its 23 nominations and 13 total wins last year shattered the records for a new comedy, making it the most-nominated debut comedy in Emmy history. That kind of dominance creates a powerful narrative for voters, especially when the show’s quality and cultural footprint remain high. Seth Rogen’s four wins for the series, tying a record for most wins by an individual in a single year, underscores the show’s across-the-board strength. The academy rarely turns its back on a reigning titan that is still delivering, positioning *The Studio* as the clear frontrunner for Outstanding Comedy Series.

## Who Are the Drama Frontrunners?

In drama, the landscape is defined by last year’s winner, **HBO’s *The Pitt***. Its victory was significant—it was the first medical procedural to win Outstanding Drama Series since *ER* in 1996. Breaking a nearly 30-year drought for the genre gives *The Pitt* a powerful claim to being more than a one-off winner; it represents a revival. With its blend of high-stakes medical drama and prestige television craftsmanship, it has established a formula that voters clearly embraced. Its main competition will likely come from the perennially nominated heavyweights like *[Severance](/streaming/best-shows-on-apple-tv/)*, which led all programs with 27 nominations last year, and *The Last of Us*, which continues to be a critical darling.

## Which Limited Series Is the Favorite?

The limited series category appears to be the most locked down. **Netflix’s *Adolescence*** isn’t just a favorite; it’s the prohibitive frontrunner. The series led all shows at last year’s ceremony with six wins, including the top prize for Outstanding Limited or Anthology Series. A performance like Owen Cooper’s, which made him the youngest male acting winner in Emmy history at age 15, creates the kind of memorable, zeitgeist-capturing moment that lingers with voters. For a show to follow up such a sweep, it would need to maintain extraordinary buzz, which *Adolescence* has managed to do.

## What New Rules Could Shake Things Up?

Beyond the shows, a key change in the rules could impact individual races. Starting with this cycle, directors or directing teams are permitted to submit multiple episodes for consideration, provided they are from different programs. This is a shift from the previous rule limiting submissions to one entry per category. This could lead to more strategic campaigning and potentially split votes in crowded fields, making races like Outstanding Directing for a Drama Series even more unpredictable. It also opens the door for versatile filmmakers to compete against themselves, which could either dilute their chances or demonstrate an overwhelming breadth of skill.

## Who Could Be This Year's Breakout Stars?

While established winners are safe bets, several performers from last year’s ceremony are now in a powerful position to repeat or break through in new ways. Tramell Tillman’s win for *Severance* made him the first Black performer to win Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Drama Series, a milestone that cements his status in the category. Similarly, Ayo Edebiri made history as the first Black woman nominated for both acting and directing in the same year for *The Bear*. Though she didn’t win last year, that dual recognition signals immense respect from the academy, making her a live contender in both fields this time around. These artists have moved from being promising nominees to established, award-winning forces.

The 2026 Emmys are shaping up to be a battle between the new guard that just arrived and the old guard trying to reclaim its throne. With viewership for the ceremony hitting a four-year high last year, there’s renewed energy around the awards. The race is on.
