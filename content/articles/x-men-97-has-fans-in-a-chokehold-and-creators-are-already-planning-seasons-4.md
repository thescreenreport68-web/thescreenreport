The creators of *[X-Men](/tv/x-men-97-season-2-to-feature-innovative-format-change/) '97* are already mapping out seasons 4 and 5, but what's really got people talking is how the show turned background players like Morph and Jubilee into total fan favorites. The reaction to season 2 has been a wave of celebration — less about big spectacle, more about the character depth that's won over even the skeptics.

## The Characters No One Saw Coming

The backbone of the buzz isn't cameos or universe-shaking events — it's the folks in the mansion. Viewers are discovering a deep, newfound love for characters who used to just blend into the background, and they're crediting the writing for making them care.

A huge chunk of the praise goes to Morph, who gets a poignant, defining arc. One user wrote, "This made me so sad. 😭 I’m glad Morph got an episode to really showcase who they are. Also, I’m glad they are showing how much of a selfish a-hole Logan can be. X-men 97 is truly a great show." The feeling is that the show isn't afraid to dive into messy, painful character dynamics. Another fan posted, "X-Men 97 was awesome this week. Writing is so good they made me actually care about Morph. For Wolverine fans, it's a special gem with some nice callbacks." For others, the excitement was straightforward: one viewer exclaimed, "Omg yay!!! Morph getting a good X-Men ‘97 episode arc this week"

Jubilee is getting the same glow-up. One viewer shared a personal connection, saying, "X-men 97 may be the best thing on TV right now. Watching it with my oldest daughter is one of my great pleasures. At S2E2, which finally featured an awesome Jubilee sequence." The show is finally giving her the spotlight she deserves, with another fan stating plainly, "X-Men 97 putting all the respect on my girl's name"

This character focus is what fans say makes the show special. They're noticing the small touches, like the evolving intro — one user pointed out, "I like how the creators of X-Men ‘97 know people aren’t skipping the intro so they make sure to add something new each time to keep fans interested." The overall mood is all about emotional investment, perfectly captured by a post that simply read, "X-MEN 97 fans after Season 2, Episode 4. ❤️‍🩹🧲"

## A Long Game Already in Motion

While fans soak up the present, the show's architects are thinking years ahead. Marvel Studios is confident, with executives and producers openly talking about a multi-season plan. Brad Winderbaum, Head of Streaming, Television, and Animation at [Marvel](/tv/marvel-officially-casts-the-next-thor/) Studios, revealed, "There's more than possibly more to come from X-Men '97. We're talking about Season 4 and 5 now. Season 3 is... I've almost seen animatics for the entire season and it is incredible. I saw an episode the other day that had me weeping in the edit bay. It's a great show."

This long-term vision comes from both passion and practicality. Producer Eric Lewald shared the enthusiasm: "Brad’s [Winderbaum] bursting up and down and you know he wants to do 10, and we want him to do 10. We want eight years from now to be having more conversations like this about season 10. Streaming is only like eight or nine, so their storytelling is more compressed than ours... So if 'X-Men '97' could do something like at least match what we did for five seasons, that would allow a lot more stories to be explored."

The team is also confident that the delays between seasons one and two are a thing of the past. Houston assured, "Luckily, the production problems won’t occur again. There was a huge gap of time between [season] one and two. They’ve learned their lessons, so with [seasons] three and four, that won’t happen again… That was a one-off."

## Why This Formula Hits Different

The match between fan reaction and creator confidence isn't random. *X-Men '97* is working because it gives a dedicated, long-time fanbase what they crave: respect for characters and their histories, delivered with modern storytelling. The positive reactions to season 2 aren't just nostalgia — they're about the show using that foundation to tell new, emotionally resonant stories. It proves that audience investment comes from character, not crisis. The show makes you care about Morph's pain or Jubilee's moment to shine, which makes you excited for what's next.

Where this is heading is clear: more seasons, more deep dives into X-Men lore, and more moments that turn background players into the heart of the story. The audience has shown it's all in, and the creators are ready to run with it.
