**Pedro Pascal revealed his personal Mount Rushmore of Nicolas Cage movies, detailed the surprisingly relaxing mechanics of his infamous Game of Thrones death, and declared New York City devoid of good Mexican food during his spicy interview on Hot Ones.**

Seated across from host Sean Evans, the star of *The Last of Us* and *The Mandalorian* tackled the show's signature lineup of increasingly hot wings. While navigating the heat, Pascal offered candid insights into his career, his fanboy tendencies, and the unique challenges of acting alongside puppets and in heavy armor.

## What Are Pedro Pascal’s Favorite Nicolas Cage Movies?

A self-professed longtime fan, Pascal didn't hesitate when asked to name his essential Nicolas Cage films. He highlighted the actor's earlier, formative work that left a lasting impression. His personal picks are *Raising Arizona*, *Peggy Sue Got Married*, *Face/Off*, and *Moonstruck*. He praised Cage's performance in *Adaptation* as potentially "one of the best screen performances in the history of American cinema," but noted the four he listed hold a special place in his development as an actor. Pascal explained that he sometimes catches himself doing something in a role and realizes, "Oh, I got that from *Vampire's Kiss* or something."

He also shared a behind-the-scenes story from filming *The Unbearable Weight of Massive Talent*, where he improvised keeping a Nicolas Cage-signed pillow in a scene despite Cage's initial objection. "I think it should stay," Pascal recalled telling him, and the moment made it into the final film.

## What Was It Really Like Filming His Game of Thrones Death?

Asked about the mechanics of shooting a scene where his character has his eyes gouged out—a fate he suffered twice on screen—Pascal gave a vivid account of filming Oberyn Martell's death in *Game of Thrones*. Contrary to the brutal on-screen result, he described the experience as cathartic and relaxing. He explained that actor Hafþór Júlíus Björnsson (The Mountain) had tubing running to his thumbs that pumped cool liquid, and the team applied fleshy, blood-filled prosthetics to Pascal's face.

"I was dead asleep, because it was so hot and this stuff was so cooling to the touch," Pascal said. He joked that he now realizes he might need to be "laid out with pieces of flesh, gelatinous, cool to the touch, face meat and pulling blood" to get a good night's sleep.

## How Does He Handle Acting Opposite Puppets Like Grogu?

Pascal discussed the unique experience of working with the Grogu puppet on *The Mandalorian*, describing it as a fulfilling scene partner. He revealed there are two technicians: one operating a remote unit for broader movements and another controlling detailed facial expressions via a wired setup he likened to "the Pentagon." The puppet's responsiveness was so effective that it genuinely moved him during emotional scenes, like saying goodbye in season two.

"This puppet is making me cry," he said, laughing about how Grogu would sometimes "steal" the scene, especially during the rare moments the Mandalorian had his helmet off.

## What Bothers Him When Watching Movies and TV Now?

Pascal admitted that his behind-the-scenes knowledge has ruined his ability to watch films and shows as a pure fan. He said his family often wants to "kill him" because he can't stop commenting on the practical realities of filming. He becomes hyper-aware of the actors' discomfort, noting things like, "Poof, I bet, you know that mud all over his body? He's freezing. Oh my gosh, that's so cool. That's a lot of blood. That's very sticky."

He specifically mentioned obsessing over the shoes actors wear, knowing they likely cause blisters, showcasing his enduring empathy for the physical toll of production.

## Where Does He Stand on the Great Tex-Mex Debate?

Growing up in San Antonio, Texas, Pascal considers himself an authority on Tex-Mex. After moving to Southern California and then spending two decades in New York City, he arrived at a definitive conclusion. When asked to break down the difference between good and bad Tex-Mex, he bluntly stated, "There's no good Mexican food in New York." He challenged anyone to "come for him" on that opinion, demonstrating his unwavering loyalty to the cuisine of his childhood.

## How Did He Handle the Spicy Wings?

Pascal approached the challenge with a mix of bravado and strategic caution, noting his spice tolerance wasn't what it used to be. He employed tactics like splitting wings and taking smaller bites. The infamous "Da' Bomb Beyond Insanity" sauce provided a significant turning point, causing him to exclaim, "Oh, no. No, no, no, no. No." He described the sensation as a "story structure" to the interview, where Evans lulls guests into a false sense of security before trying to "kill" them. Despite the heat, he powered through, even musing about the location of newfound nerve endings around his lips.
