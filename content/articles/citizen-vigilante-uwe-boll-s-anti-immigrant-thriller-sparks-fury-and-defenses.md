Uwe Boll’s return to filmmaking with *Citizen Vigilante* has ignited a firestorm. The anti-immigrant thriller, amplified by Elon Musk, stars Armie Hammer in a role he now calls “hateful, disgusting” — and the audience is just as split. The film, which follows a landlord who takes to murdering immigrants and Muslims, has become a flashpoint, drawing condemnation from critics and many viewers while finding a smaller, vocal set of defenders.

## Defenders and Disgust: The Split Is Real

The online reaction to *Citizen Vigilante* is a study in deep polarization. On one side, a segment of viewers champions the film as a necessary, unfiltered take. One user wrote, "Still, fans of CITIZEN VIGILANTE." This sentiment is echoed by director Uwe Boll himself, who in an email argued, "It was clear that the film will split the audience but I never cared about reviewers – I care about the audience.

But the far louder and more numerous response has been one of condemnation and ridicule. Many viewers were immediately struck by the film's bizarre, on-the-nose premise. One viewer posted, "Wait, there’s a film directed by Uwe Boll in which Armie Hamer stalks through a country called Europe killing illegal immigrants and speaking entirely in Elon Musk’s talking points. Oh, and also he’s a FUCKING LANDLORD? That’s just so precious. Who wrote that shit? Grok?!"

Others dismissed the film as low-quality propaganda. "Citizen Vigilante is conservative slop," one user wrote. Another simply stated, "i watched citizen vigilante and this might genuinely be Uwe's worst film to date." The criticism often extends beyond the filmmaking to its perceived message, with one commenter noting, "Musk’s amplification of the movie 'Citizen Vigilante' legitimizes violence against immigrants."

## Hammer’s Hasty Walk-Back

A central thread in the conversation is the stark reversal from the film's star, Armie Hammer. After taking the role in a bid to work again—"I would have done a f*cking cat food commercial. I just wanted to work again," he said in an interview—Hammer has publicly and forcefully denounced the final product. In a statement, he called it "F*ck. This is hateful, disgusting," and separately clarified, "That was not the movie I thought we made." A source close to the actor explained, "When he saw the final product, he was, ‘That was not the movie I thought we made.’"

## Why This Film Is Landing Like a Grenade

The intensity of the *Citizen Vigilante* controversy stems from a perfect storm of elements. First, its content taps directly into real-world, heated anxieties about immigration, framed in an exploitative, violent thriller format. Second, the involvement of Elon Musk, who posted the full film on X, gave it massive exposure far beyond its expected niche, forcing it into the mainstream conversation. Third, the casting of Armie Hammer—an actor whose own career is mired in scandal—adds a layer of meta-commentary and irony that viewers can’t ignore.

Critics have been unsparing. One described it as "Trashy and fashy," while another wrote, "'Citizen Vigilante' is the cinematic equivalent of a fascist state where the trains don't even run on time." Journalist Giancarlo Sopo summarized a common critical take: "Citizen Vigilante is the worst movie of 2026. The bad filmmaking is the least of its sins. It uses real anxieties about migration policies to make you cheer for the murder of cops and innocent civilians."

The film's notoriety is further cemented by its rejection in Germany, where the FSK refused classification, effectively banning its release. Despite this, or perhaps because of it, Boll is already teasing a sequel, posting online, "CV2 will be different ….and playing in the USA."

The conversation now is less about the quality of the film—most agree it's poorly made—and more about what it represents and who is embracing it. Is it, as some defenders claim, a cathartic outlet for a disaffected audience? Or is it, as its detractors fear, a dangerous piece of propaganda given a megaphone by one of the world's most influential people? The debate rages on, ensuring that *Citizen Vigilante*, for all its artistic failures, will be remembered as a cultural flashpoint.
