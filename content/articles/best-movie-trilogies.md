If you want the short answer, here it is: **The Lord of the Rings** is the benchmark every other contender is measured against, and it sits at the top of almost any honest list of the **best movie trilogies** ever made. Peter Jackson's Middle-earth saga was shot back-to-back, released across three consecutive Decembers, and ended with *The Return of the King* sweeping the Oscars. Nothing else has matched that combination of scale, consistency, and payoff.

But a great trilogy is about more than one crown. The films below all share a rarer quality: they were built as a single escalating story, not a string of sequels chasing a hit. Below is our ranking, with what earns each its place.

## The Lord of the Rings sets the gold standard

Released in 2001, 2002, and 2003, *The Fellowship of the Ring*, *The Two Towers*, and *The Return of the King* function as one continuous epic. Because Jackson filmed them together in New Zealand across a single continuous shoot, the tone, cast, and visual world never drift. The story deepens as it goes — the stakes widen, the fellowship fractures and reunites, and the final chapter pays off threads planted hours earlier. *The Return of the King* won 11 Academy Awards, tying *Ben-Hur* and *Titanic* for the most wins by a single film, and it was the first fantasy film to take Best Picture. For sheer ambition realized, this is the one to beat.

## The Dark Knight trilogy reinvented the superhero arc

Christopher Nolan's Batman films — *Batman Begins*, *The Dark Knight*, and *The Dark Knight Rises* — proved a comic-book franchise could carry the weight of serious drama. The middle film, anchored by Heath Ledger's Oscar-winning Joker, is the high point and one of the most acclaimed blockbusters of its era. What makes the run a true trilogy is structure: it tracks Bruce Wayne from origin to symbol to closure, giving the story an actual ending rather than an open door for more. If you want to see how Nolan's craft evolved across his career, our [Christopher Nolan movies ranked](/movies/christopher-nolan-movies-ranked) guide places these films in context.

## Toy Story showed animation could carry a complete saga

Pixar's *Toy Story*, *Toy Story 2*, and *Toy Story 3* form one of the most emotionally complete trilogies in any medium. The original broke ground as the first fully computer-animated feature, the second deepened its themes of loyalty and obsolescence, and *Toy Story 3* delivered a finale about letting go that moved a generation that had grown up with Woody and Buzz. The franchise later continued, but those first three films work as a self-contained arc with a genuine ending.

## The original Star Wars trilogy defined the modern blockbuster

The films now known as *A New Hope*, *The Empire Strikes Back*, and *Return of the Jedi* reshaped Hollywood. *Empire* is routinely cited as one of the greatest sequels ever made — darker, more complex, and home to one of cinema's most famous twists. Together the three movies established the template for franchise filmmaking that the industry still follows, and they remain a touchstone for how a middle chapter can elevate an entire trilogy.

## Before is the trilogy that proved you don't need spectacle

Richard Linklater's *Before Sunrise*, *Before Sunset*, and *Before Midnight* are the quiet outlier on this list and, for many critics, among the most rewarding. Released across nearly two decades — from 1995 to 2013 — and following the same two characters as they visibly age, the films trade explosions for conversation. The result is a portrait of a relationship that few franchises of any kind have matched for honesty.

## A few more that belong in the conversation

Trilogy debates rarely end, and several runs deserve a mention even when they miss the top tier:

- **The Godfather** — Parts I and II are widely held to be among the greatest films ever made; the third is divisive, which keeps the whole from ranking higher as a unit.
- **Indiana Jones (original three)** — *Raiders of the Lost Ark* through *The Last Crusade* set the standard for adventure filmmaking.
- **The Matrix (original)** — a genre-defining first film whose sequels split audiences.
- **Cornetto / Three Flavours** — Edgar Wright's *Shaun of the Dead*, *Hot Fuzz*, and *The World's End*, linked by theme rather than plot.

## What separates a great trilogy from three good films

The pattern across this list is consistency of vision. The strongest trilogies were either planned as one story or guided by a filmmaker with a clear endgame, which is why they escalate instead of repeat. A weak third act — or a film bolted on after the fact — is what keeps otherwise beloved series out of the top tier. The best trilogies earn their endings.

## The bottom line

There is no single correct ranking, but the consensus is durable: *The Lord of the Rings* leads, *The Dark Knight* and *Toy Story* trilogies sit just behind, and the original *Star Wars* and *Before* films round out a top tier defined by storytelling that builds to something. The rest of the conversation is the fun part.
