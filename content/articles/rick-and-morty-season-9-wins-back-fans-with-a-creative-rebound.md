The response has been overwhelmingly positive — genuine delight, sharp observations, and a few lingering grievances, but the dominant mood is celebration.

## The Jokes, The Character Beats, The Joy: What's Clicking

The reaction was instant — and loud. Viewers are pointing to a density of jokes, clever character work, and a general sense that the show is having fun again. One of the most universally praised bits involves Jerry Smith being magically muted, only to immediately lean into the situation with gusto.

One fan summed it up: "I like that instead of getting mad for being muted, Jerry was just having fun pretending to be a mime."

Another fan added: "Losing it at Jerry immediately getting excited and miming when he got muted lmfao."

This appreciation for character-based humor extends to the show's evolving dynamic. A major running thread this season is Morty having the empathy center of his brain eaten by a parasite. Fans are finding his newly callous attitude both logical and funny. One viewer observed that Morty's lack of empathy makes his attitude more logical.

Another chimed in: "I love how Morty has no empathy now because a intergalactic parasite ate the empathy center in his brain, but Summer is just Summer."

Specific visual gags are also landing perfectly. A moment where Rick magically summons Morty, who comes flying through the air screaming, has been repeatedly compared to Thor summoning his hammer, Mjolnir.

One fan noted: "The substitution Jutsu followed by Morty getting Mjolnir'd is hilarious,"

Another viewer elaborated: "I was wondering what Rick was doing and then heard Morty’s scream get louder and realized he was getting summoned like Mjolnir."

These moments have led to a broader sentiment that the season is a high point. "This has been one of the best seasons of [Rick and Morty](/tv/rick-and-morty-season-9-promises-a-full-tank-but-fans-are-still-split-on-the/) in a long time," one user stated plainly, a feeling echoed by many.

## Morale Shift: How a Revitalized Writers Room Sparked the Comeback

This revitalized feeling isn't accidental. In interviews, co-creator Dan Harmon has openly discussed the high morale on the team following a period of internal instability. His comments resonate with viewers who sense a change in the show's atmosphere.

Harmon offered: "This season in a lot of ways is a celebration, and I think you can feel it in everything. Everyone was so grateful that this show held together and weathered all the storms it did internally. All our best people stayed with us. The morale was through the roof, and you could feel it in every department, and it bled into every episode."

He doubled down, arguing the season's energy defies its number: "That's why it feels so dense and upbeat. Everyone was so thrilled being part of this thing. It just doesn’t feel like the ninth season of a show; it feels like the third season of a show."

## Not Everyone Is Buying It — But the Positives Drown Out the Noise

Amid the praise, a minor but vocal undercurrent of criticism persists. This largely falls into two categories: lingering association with past controversies and a lament that the show has lost its broader cultural relevance.

One negative post, which received little engagement, attempted to tie current fans to the ousted co-creator's actions, asking: "How many of them are Rick and Morty fans who defended Justin Roiland after he was exposed as a pedophile?"

Another viewer expressed a more melancholic critique: "I feel bad for Rick and Morty. Season 9 has been amazing so far and no one cares. They've never recovered from being 'the Pickle Rick show'."

These posts are outliers. The conversation is overwhelmingly focused on dissecting new jokes and celebrating the show's creative resurgence. The talk now is less about whether the show can survive without its original co-creator and more about which specific gag from the latest episode was the funniest — a return to normalcy that many fans have wanted.
