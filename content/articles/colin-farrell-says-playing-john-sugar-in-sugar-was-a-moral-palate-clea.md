Colin Farrell described his role as the empathetic private investigator John Sugar as a refreshing change of pace after embodying the ruthless gangster Oswald Cobblepot.

Speaking to Robin Roberts on *Good Morning America*, the actor explained that stepping into the shoes of the kind-hearted alien detective for Apple TV+'s **Sugar** served as a moral reset. He had just finished a grueling run as the villain in HBO's *The Penguin*, a role that earned him a Golden Globe but left him, by his own admission, kind of hating the character.

## Why was Sugar a different kind of role for Farrell?

The core appeal of John Sugar, according to Farrell, is his unwavering belief in human decency. The character works in the shadows of Los Angeles, witnessing violence and the uglier aspects of humanity, yet he remains convinced that beneath the trauma, people are fundamentally good. Farrell finds this perspective a lovely place to work from each day, a stark contrast to the brutal ambition of Oz Cobb.

He framed the show's central sci-fi twist—that Sugar is an alien—as a narrative device to explore universal human themes. The story tackles loneliness, the search for belonging, and the impact of loss, as Sugar himself is driven by the disappearance of his sister.

## What does Farrell say about filming in Los Angeles?

Farrell was emphatic about the importance of shooting *Sugar* in its actual setting. He called Los Angeles a magic, spirited, and multicultural place, praising its art scene, bookstores, and wildlife. Filming there allowed the California-based crew to sleep in their own beds instead of having to relocate for tax incentives.

To be there, to be in our own beds, for the crew not to have to leave their families, he said. The actor views the show as a bit of a love letter to the city itself, with LA acting as a prominent character, not just a backdrop.

## Is The Penguin coming back for more?

When asked about reviving his acclaimed DC villain, Farrell expressed clear interest. He loved playing the character and was thrilled by the audience's extraordinary response to the HBO series. The actor stated he would love to do a second and third season if the creative justification was there.

He specifically referenced a harrowing scene in episode 7 as a moment that made even him hate the character. For any return, he said the script and idea would need to be strong enough—if the pen clicks.

## What can he reveal about The Batman Part II?

Farrell was characteristically tight-lipped about plot details for Matt Reeves's sequel, joking that his life would be in peril if he said anything. He did, however, offer significant praise for the project. He called the script extraordinary and full of heart, noting that his role is small, which he sees as a benefit.

I'm only in a couple of scenes, which is wonderful cuz it means the movie isn't ruined for me, he explained. Farrell will join the production in London in a couple of weeks for what he described as a little moment.
