Recent box office hits are demonstrating a clear audience preference for films emphasizing practical effects and challenging, old-school filmmaking over CGI-heavy franchise entries, according to a report in The Hollywood Reporter. The analysis points to the success of films like [Christopher Nolan](/movies/a-few-thousand-bucks-vs-250-million-the-ai-odyssey-that-s-splitting-the-internet/)'s "The Odyssey" and others as evidence of this trend.

## What Movies Are Proving This Trend?

The report specifically highlights "The Odyssey," "Project Hail Mary," "Backrooms," and "[Obsession](/movies/box-office-obsession-surpasses-400-million-globally/)." These films not only heavily relied on practical effects but communicated their old-school moviemaking efforts in their marketing. For "The Odyssey," Nolan and his cast spoke extensively about filming at tough-to-reach real-world locations, with the director refusing to use CGI for set extensions.

Nolan told The Hollywood Reporter, “I definitely hit the limits of my own stamina and everybody’s stamina. I mean, it’s The Odyssey, of course it should be difficult. We’re not doing the job right making a film of The Odyssey if it doesn’t seem difficult.”

## What Movies Are Struggling?

Conversely, the report notes that several franchise films with built-in audiences have been criticized for looking overtly artificial. The trailers for box office disappointments "Supergirl," "Star Wars: The Mandalorian and Grogu," and "Moana" all notably looked, well, a bit fakey. A viral Reddit comment about a recent superhero trailer asked, “Is anyone else concerned how ‘filmed in a cupboard’ this looks?”

## Why Does This Matter Now?

The analysis suggests that in an era where AI-generated content is widespread, audiences are placing a higher value on authenticity and tangible craftsmanship. The story behind a film's difficult creation is becoming part of its appeal. Furthermore, the report notes a refreshing surge for non-franchise movies, with several original titles ranking among the year's top domestic box office earners.

While declaring a permanent trend is risky, the current box office tea leaves, according to the report, indicate moviegoers will show up for compelling, handcrafted stories from passionate filmmakers.
