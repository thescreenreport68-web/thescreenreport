After the global, club-conquering dominance of her 'Brat' era, [Charli XCX](/music/charli-xcx-directs-chaos-in-camera-music-video-starring-vincent-cassel/) is deliberately pulling back. She’s trading stadium-sized projections for something more intimate, personal, and private. In her own words, she’s entering a new phase focused on creative fulfillment rather than repeating past successes. This post-Brat pivot, encompassing new music, fashion, and film, has landed with a thud—but not the kind that shakes a dancefloor. Instead, it’s split her audience right down the middle, sparking a debate about artistic growth, authenticity, and the uncomfortable edges of her new collaborations.

## The Intimate Pivot: Fans Who Are Here for the Quiet
For a significant portion of her fanbase, this shift isn't a retreat but a progression. They see an artist being transparent about the pressures of pop stardom and choosing a path that feels genuine. This era is being read as a coherent, self-aware evolution that proves life—and art—can get better after 30.

One user wrote, "This era is soooo honest and straightforward about what it means to be a female pop artist and songwriter. The impossible expectation to be both sexually dominant and powerful but also cute and submissive and naive… men can be as nasty as they want but"

Another viewer posted, "Charli is single-handedly proving that life literally BEGINS at 30 like... look at her??? 🔥"

The artist herself has framed this move as a necessary step. In a recent interview, Charli XCX stated, "I knew when I was making it that I was never going to make that record again. It’s not creatively rewarding for me to make the same thing twice." She later added, "I want to do things that fulfil me as an artist and a person. And I think sometimes it's hard to follow that inner voice. There are a lot of distractions around."

This commitment to a new, more private approach resonates with fans who appreciate the maturity. A fan online said, "she's not afraid to be sexy and corny at the same time, and a lot of artists try just doing one and not committing!! charli really is just another level"

The reaction is also peppered with the self-aware, often horny humor that defines much of her fan discourse. One post captured the tone perfectly: "Seeing Charli finger herself wasn't in my 2026 bingo card but slay 😍" Another added, "She just used the horniest memes from Twitter and put them in her music video. ETERNAL MOTHER"

## The Cringe and The Controversy
Not everyone is onboard with the new vibe. Alongside the praise is a palpable undercurrent of discomfort. Some audience posts express confusion or mild disappointment with the new material's aesthetic and execution, finding it awkward or alienating compared to the high-energy cohesion of 'Brat.'

A viewer noted, "All these locations & outfits for a 2 minute song. Oh charli." Another simply stated, "I'm gonna get fired for watching this at work," highlighting the sometimes jarring intimacy of the new visuals.

However, the most pointed criticism—and the ugly side of the reaction—centers on a collaborator. The music video for "Camera" features a voiceover and cameo from French actor Vincent Cassel. This choice has ignited significant anger because of Cassel's past public defense of the controversial influencer Andrew Tate, who is facing charges of human trafficking and rape. For many fans, this association is a bridge too far, turning an artistic choice into a moral affront. The comment sections turned into a battleground.

The controversy is a dominant thread in the discourse, summarized in one post: "Vincent Cassel dans le clip de Charli XCX : la colère des fans expliquée ❓ Pourquoi les fans de Charli XCX sont en colère ? ❓ Vincent Cassel a-t-il défendu Andrew Tate ? ❓ Qui a réalisé le clip « Camera » de Charli XCX"

It's disheartening to see the reaction curdle into outright anger over this association. This isn't mere criticism of a musical direction; it's a deeper, more uncomfortable reckoning with the company an artist keeps.

## Why the Divide Runs So Deep
The Charli XCX post-Brat era fan reaction is so split because it touches on fundamental questions about an artist's trajectory. 'Brat' was a cultural monolith—inescapable, unifying, and massively scaled. To deliberately step away from that blueprint is, to some, a brave act of authenticity. To others, it feels like a rejection of what made her moment so powerful. The timeline did not stay calm for long.

Charli anticipated this division. She posted, "still marketing but it’s just a different approach, more intimate, personal, private, one on one. less about the projection of scale," and in an interview admitted, "We know there’s gonna be people who are bothered by it, but that’s fine."

The controversy around Vincent Cassel adds a layer of ethical discomfort that pure artistic critique doesn't capture. It forces the conversation beyond taste and into the murkier waters of accountability and association, which is always a more volatile mix.

## What Happens When the Noise Fades?
The conversation is now a tug-of-war between two visions: one that champions an artist's right to evolve intimately and on her own terms, and another that mourns a lost collective energy or takes issue with her collaborators. The Vincent Cassel controversy ensures the discourse won't be purely about aesthetics; it will remain partly a debate about the lines between art and artist, and the weight of an artist's associations.

Charli XCX has stated she's trying to step off the internet more this year. But as she builds this smaller, more personal world, the noise from the outside—both celebratory and critical—shows no sign of quieting down. The success of this new era may ultimately depend on whether her audience learns to appreciate the quiet intimacy she's offering, or if they keep longing for the roar of the club.
