After years of waiting, the *Scary Movie* franchise is back—and the internet did what it does best. The long-awaited sequel/reboot, which brings back original cast members from the first two films, has sparked a deeply divided reaction online, with fans nostalgic for the original Wayans-era humor clashing directly with viewers who find the new film a cluttered, plotless mess.

## The Nostalgia Crowd Is Loving the Chaos
For a significant portion of the audience, *Scary Movie 2026* is exactly the dose of chaotic, no-holds-barred comedy they've been missing. These fans are celebrating what they see as a direct return to the spirit of the early 2000s parodies, complete with the rapid-fire jokes and absurdity that defined the franchise's peak.

One user wrote, "Wayans getting their franchise back had us hyped with high expectations. This is a fun movie full of jokes and many references from many movies."

The sentiment is that if you loved the first two movies, you'll find plenty to enjoy here. Another viewer posted, "It was absolutely freaking hilarious from the start to finish. I love how the og characters from Scary Movie 1 and 2 came back from this film." For these fans, the heavy use of modern pop culture references—from contemporary movies to big events—is a feature, not a bug. As one fan online said, "I like that if you’ve seen both of the original two movies, you’ll get the jokes because the cast was from the first movies. Also they did good job on making fun of movies and big events from the past and up to the current events happening now."

The excitement has even spilled over into hopes for future projects, with one post capturing a common desire: "And I hope more people will watch it during opening weekend, so that way the Wayans brothers can create White Chicks 2."

## The Backlash: 'No Coherent Plot Whatsoever'
On the other side of the aisle, the reaction has curdled into outright disappointment. The most consistent and scathing criticism targets the film's structure—or lack thereof. Many viewers argue it feels less like a parody movie and more like a string of disconnected skits.

A fan online posted a particularly harsh take that sums up this camp: "SCARY MOVIE might genuinely be the worst movie I’ve seen in theaters. As a big of the original trilogy, I couldn’t have been more disappointed in this absolute mess. I know it’s a goofy parody movie, but there’s literally no coherent plot whatsoever."

This critique of a missing narrative spine is everywhere in the negative reactions. Another viewer said, "I wish it was more along the lines of the previous Scary Movies with some kind of a base plot. This was just randomly thrown together. For waiting so many years for a follow up, this was a let down." The comparison to a certain animated show's style of humor came up repeatedly, with one post stating, "The references weren’t meshed into the story at all. They became Family Guy cutaways. It was dreadful. I laughed at some points but the cringe of the rest just overwhelmed the film."

Some of the criticism carries an extra layer of personal letdown. One user wrote, "I knew from the trailer it was cheeks, but I wanted to support black excellence, and this wasn’t it."

## The Middle Ground: Funny, But Flawed
Between the passionate love and outright hate exists a large, pragmatic middle. These viewers acknowledge the film's fun moments and clever nods but can't ignore its glaring flaws, often wishing it had been executed better.

One user captured this mixed feeling perfectly: "It could have been better. I laughed some. People were missing. Jokes were lost. Shannon Elizabeth should have had a role. There were a lot of references to contemporary pop culture."

For them, the movie's attempt to cram in too much modern culture is its Achilles' heel. Another viewer said, "As much as I love Scary Movie , this was ok. It had its cringe moments and funny moments. But I feel as if they stuffed a bit too much modern culture into the movie." The consensus here is that the nostalgia and laughs are real, but weighed down by a cluttered execution. As one fan online posted, "Wasn’t bad but wasn’t great, though they do imply possible other ways we can get more from the same folks. There’s a mid-credits scene. They punched in almost every direction but mostly on current culture. I like the nostalgia but wished it could’ve flowed better."

## Why the Discourse Isn't Cooling Off
This split makes sense when you consider what *Scary Movie* represents. For many, the first two films are untouchable comedy classics—a specific brand of anarchic, reference-heavy humor tied directly to the Wayans brothers. The 2026 film explicitly tries to recapture that, bringing back original cast and doubling down on rapid-fire jokes. For viewers who just want that nostalgic feeling, it works.

But parody has evolved. Modern audiences, perhaps conditioned by more structured satires, seem less patient with a film that prioritizes joke density over narrative coherence. The criticism that it feels like "Family Guy cutaways" is particularly telling; it suggests the humor exists in a vacuum, not woven into a story. This fundamental clash—between fans seeking chaotic fun and viewers expecting a more polished product—is at the heart of every *Scary Movie 2026* fan reaction.

The conversation isn't ending with the credits, either. Mentions of a mid-credits scene have fans speculating about future installments, and the loud calls for a White Chicks 2 show that audience appetite for the Wayans' style remains strong, even if this particular offering left some viewers cold. The franchise is back, but it's clear it will have to win over a skeptical segment of its own fanbase to truly reclaim its throne.
