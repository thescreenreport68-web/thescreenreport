After a two-year wait, fans were eager to see where the dystopian drama would take its heroine, Juliette Nichols. The answer, it seems, is backward: the trailer heavily implies Juliette has lost her memory after the fiery season 2 finale. For a significant portion of the audience, this creative choice feels less like a bold narrative turn and more like a frustrating, predictable stall.

## Fans wasted no time calling it a ‘waste’
The dominant reaction online is one of profound disappointment. For many, the amnesia trope represents a creative step backward, a device that will force the show to spend precious episodes retreading ground the audience already knows. The sentiment is that after such a long hiatus, the story should be charging forward, not hitting the reset button on its protagonist’s knowledge.

One user wrote, “I had such high hopes for season 3 but the 'memory lost' trope to keep the story from progressing is so disappointing.”

The frustration is compounded by the two-year gap between seasons. Another viewer posted, “Two years in between seasons that lead into an amnesia/flashback plot—is not doing the show any favors.” The wait has heightened expectations, making a perceived stalling tactic feel like a particular letdown.

The core criticism is that the plot will burn narrative time without advancing the story. A fan online said, “so how many EPs are we gonna waste on her reminding pieces of what we already saw on prev seasons? :/” This fear of repetition is a common thread, with another comment reading, “The whole new season looks like a regurgitation of what we already know. Writers took the season off apparently.”

The reaction isn't just disappointment; for some, it’s outright exasperation. One post captured the visceral reaction simply: “NOT F'ING MEMORY LOSS R U KIDDING ME?!?! WHY???”

## The other side is holding out hope
Despite the tidal wave of negativity, a smaller but passionate contingent of viewers is pushing back, championing the series and its star. Their argument hinges on the show’s established quality and the powerhouse at its center, Rebecca Ferguson.

For these fans, *Silo* represents the pinnacle of modern television sci-fi. One user argued, “Apple TV is single-handedly carrying the entire weight of modern premium sci-fi on its back. Silo, Severance, For All Mankind... absolute gold standard.”

Much of this unwavering support is directed at Ferguson herself, who both stars in and produces the series. “Rebecca Ferguson already is an absolute legend. She is such an insanely good actress,” one fan posted. Another elaborated on her dual role, writing, “Silo is the perfect vehicle for Rebecca Ferguson to lead her own show. Not only does she star in it, but she also serves as a producer, which makes it feel just as much like her show as it is the creator of the show.” This group is willing to trust that the creative team, with Ferguson’s involvement, will handle the amnesia arc in a compelling way, seeing it as a minor bump in an otherwise stellar journey.

## A tired trope in a premium package
So why is this landing so poorly? The *[Silo season 3](/tv/silo-season-3-channels-classic-thrillers-for-political-plot/) amnesia controversy* taps into a deep-seated audience fatigue with memory-loss plots. In an era of prestige television, viewers expect sophisticated, propulsive storytelling. The amnesia device is often viewed as a hackneyed shortcut, a way to create artificial mystery and drag out a resolution. When a show like *Silo*, which has built its reputation on tense, intelligent world-building, appears to employ it, the disconnect feels stark. The fear isn’t just about a single plot point; it’s that the show might be abandoning the very qualities that made it special in favor of a network-TV-style stall.

The conversation is now squarely focused on execution. Can the writers use this trope to reveal new character depths or explore the story’s mythology in a fresh way? Or will it simply be, as one disillusioned fan put it, “an entire season of the characters figuring out things they already figured out and we already know.... rivetting...” The burden of proof is firmly on the show. The trailer promised amnesia; the season will need to deliver a reason for it that justifies the audience’s patience and surpasses their decidedly low expectations.
