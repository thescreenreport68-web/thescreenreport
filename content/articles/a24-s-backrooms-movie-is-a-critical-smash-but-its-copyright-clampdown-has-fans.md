The *[Backrooms](/movies/backrooms-box-office-day-50-adds-215-180-as-domestic-total-hits-195-5-million/)* movie is finally here, and the verdict from horror fans is in: it's a masterclass in slow-burn dread. But that victory lap hit a major snag, as studio A24's wave of copyright strikes against fan creations threatens to overshadow the film's success and alienate the very community that built the mythos.

## From Creepypasta to Cinematic Triumph

For viewers, the film nailed the unsettling, liminal-space vibe they were hoping for. The praise is all about its patient, skin-crawling atmosphere and the clever depth it finds in the concept.

One user wrote, "Its a big metaphor for mental health issues, unresolved trauma, pushing emotions down, and then being consumed by your own psychological hell. It was an incredible watch."

This reading of the film's monsters found a lot of agreement. "I interpreted the Clark demon eating Clark, was Clarks mental health problems consuming him. I love the metaphor," posted another viewer.

Certain scenes have already become legendary for their sheer creep factor. "It’s so rare nowadays for a movie to genuinely creep me out but THAT Christmas tree scene… Jesus. Absolutely loved this movie," said a fan. "I found the pirate way more unnerving after they revealed him. His design is a part of this, his face looks confused, suggesting the entity isn't malicious. And I feel they really nailed the uncanny valley," one viewer noted.

Even the deliberate, slow pacing got a passionate defense from supporters. "13:25 I got full heartedly disagree with this, the slow paced pans through the backrooms were incredible. I was in the theater literally grinning ear to ear every hallway shot, it was beautiful," argued a supporter.

## When Celebration Turns to Copyright Chaos

While the artistic praise flowed, a much angrier conversation exploded online. Fans started reporting that A24 was issuing takedowns against a huge range of Backrooms content—from wallpapers to indie games—that existed long before the movie.

The reaction was instant — and loud, fueled by a sense of betrayal given the Backrooms' roots as a public-domain creepypasta. One furious fan posted, "Ladies and gentlemen, meet this fuckass studio A24, a Backrooms fan account posted . ‘- first, they were copyright striking BACKROOMS themed items and wallpaper designs - and now, they are copyright striking Indie Backrooms Games. I SWEAR, A24 thinks they OWN Backrooms. A24 doesn’t deserve [Kane Parsons](/movies/kane-parsons-sparks-multi-million-dollar-studio-bidding-wars-after-gen-z/), tbh."

The feeling that a studio is cashing in on a community-built idea is everywhere. "Apple Cheated! Creators built a public domain world that fans adored. Evil Apple Backrooms took the ideas, made a movie, then banned the creator’s work," read another critical post, voicing a fear that's been bubbling since the project was announced.

Another user put it even more bluntly: "A24 is issuing takedowns for all sorts of backrooms content on social media, as well as merch elsewhere right now, that have nothing to do with the movie. I feel like a lot of people were afraid this would happen. They just... own the backrooms."

## Damage Control Mode: Activate

As the **Backrooms movie controversy** grew, director and original creator Kane Parsons stepped in to try and calm things down. On social media, he initially stated, "I’m looking into this. Should not be happening."

A24 itself put out a statement on Instagram trying to douse the flames: "A24 makes no claim of ownership over the yellow wallpaper, the original post referencing it, or any of the community works that have since been built around it. We will continue to support the artists who, like Kane, were inspired by it."

Despite these attempts to soothe, Parsons' own past interviews now sound prophetic. He's previously called aggressive copyright enforcement "genuinely harmful to creativity" and a symptom of "cultural and economic rot."—quotes that hit differently for fans now on the receiving end of those takedowns.

## The Great Fan Divide

This isn't really a fight about the movie's quality, which most people love. It's a clash between two worlds: one that judges art on its own terms, and another that can't separate it from the open-source, collaborative spirit where it was born. For many, the Backrooms is a shared idea. Seeing a major studio use legal tools against fan work—even if blamed on a "system error"—feels like a core violation. It turns the film from a tribute into a takeover.

Meanwhile, the deep-dive discussions about the movie's plot are still going strong. Fans are obsessed with unpacking its mysteries, like the origin of that pirate entity.

As things stand, the path forward is messy. The film is a hit, but it's left a chunk of its core audience feeling burned. Whether future adaptations of community myths can succeed may depend on studios learning to honor the source without alienating the source material's biggest fans. For now, the discourse isn't cooling off any time soon, split right down the middle between love for the movie and fury at the studio behind it.
