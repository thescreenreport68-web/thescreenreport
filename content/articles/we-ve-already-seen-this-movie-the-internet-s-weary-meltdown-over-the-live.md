Disney finally released that live-action [Moana](/movies/catherine-laga-aia-and-dwayne-johnson-lead-moana-live-action-cast/) trailer, and cue the meltdown — but not the nostalgic kind. The internet's reaction is a masterclass in déjà vu fatigue, with exhaustion and open mockery dominating the conversation. Audiences are questioning why this remake exists at all, while a handful of defenders clutch at the promise of a familiar song.

## Groundhog Day at Sea

The most consistent gripe? The trailer offers absolutely nothing new — just a grayer, less impressive version of a story everyone already knows by heart. This isn't just disappointment; it's outright ridicule at what feels like a total lack of effort.

One user put it bluntly: "Disney: Hey, want a visually less appealing version of the same exact story shot for shot?"

Another viewer chimed in: "They practically showed us whole movie in one trailer. Oh wait we've already seen the whole movie haven't we?"

This feeling of creative bankruptcy extends to the technical approach. Many argue the heavy reliance on CGI defeats the entire premise of a "live-action" adaptation. It is not live action, it is fully animated lol.

Even the aesthetic choices came under fire. One person noted: "Why are movies so gray now. Even the pacific islands look like Scotland. It almost looks black and white."

## The Rock’s Biceps vs. The Bottom Line

[Dwayne Johnson](/celebrity/wil-wheaton-slams-coward-dwayne-the-rock-johnson-over-keeping-his-poli/)’s return as Maui was expected, but it became a lightning rod for criticism about Hollywood’s remake economy. His involvement reads less like a passionate reprisal and more like a transactional check.

One user quipped: "The Rock's most anticipated paycheck since the last one."

One post celebrated: "Hearing that the Moana live action movie did horribly brings me hope lol." Another framed it as a unifying moment: "disney fans and disney haters alike unite over the moana live action movie bombing in the box office. this is a net positive for society."

## Shiny Lights and Nostalgia Defenders

Amid the sea of negativity, a vocal minority found things to praise, often latching onto specific nostalgic details. The confirmed return of Jemaine Clement as the vain crab Tamatoa was a bright spot.

One viewer noted: "One thing I noticed is Jemaine Clement is back to voice Tamatoa in this live action too." Another excitedly posted: "Oh my goodness. They got the same guy to voice the crab. That's 2 songs that are gonna have the same vocals."

Some also appreciated the meta-humor in the trailer itself, with one person pointing out: "'It's true, I have beautiful hair.' I see what you did there, Disney." A few argued the film is being unfairly maligned, with one defender writing: "I honestly don’t get why people say the live action moana movie is ass. It was amazing!!! They changed a bit of the dialog and scenes, but not too much. They did such a good job turning it into live action. I recommend!"

## The Ironic Chorus

Beyond the praise and scorn, a segment of the audience reacted with wry, observational humor. They pointed out the ironic timing and strange logic of Hollywood’s remake schedule.

One neutral observer cracked: "'It's ten minutes longer. It feels 30 minutes longer, but it's 10 minutes longer.'" Another highlighted the strange passage of time, posting: "'If you had a kid when moana was released, that kid would still be a kid...'"

Critic and commentator Lindsay Ellis offered a similarly pointed observation, noting: "There is something very funny to me how Rachel House who played Moana’s grandma in Moana, a 12-year-old movie, is still too young now to play her grandma in the remake."

## The Breaking Point

The intensity of the backlash isn’t just about this one film — it’s about reaching a breaking point. The remake offers no compelling artistic reason for being — no bold new interpretation, no technological leap — just a grayer, CGI-heavy photocopy. It has become the perfect symbol for a corporate strategy audiences now view as creatively bankrupt. The conversation has moved past judging the trailer’s merits to using the film as evidence in a larger argument: that Disney’s live-action remake machine has finally run out of gas, and the audience is done playing along.

The final word online seems to be one of dismissal and a plea for originality. As one user perfectly summarized the prevailing mood: "hear me out... what if they made a cartoon version of this"

One thing is certain: nobody is done talking about this.
