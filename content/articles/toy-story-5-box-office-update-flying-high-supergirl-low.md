[Pixar](/movies/toy-story-5-eyes-300m-box-office-supergirl-underwhelms/)’s latest adventure is proving that old toys still have plenty of play left in them, while a new superhero is having trouble getting off the ground. adline, *Toy Story 5* is holding onto the number one spot at the domestic box office in its second weekend. Early estimates have the Andrew Stanton-directed film earning around $74 million, a figure that will push its total domestic haul over the $300 million mark.

That momentum confirms the film’s status as a summer juggernaut. It’s a vital win for Pixar, which invested a reported $250 million in the production. The film’s global total now stands at [$379 million worldwide](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/).

## How Did Toy Story 5 Open?

Its launch was historic. The film earned $159.7 million domestically and $150.5 million internationally in its first three days. That combined $310.2 million global debut is now the biggest opening weekend in Pixar’s history, not adjusted for inflation. It surpassed the previous franchise record held by *Toy Story 4* ($244.5 million) and the studio high set by *Inside Out 2* ($294.2 million).

The strong start was fueled by positive word-of-mouth. Critics gave it a [93% on Rotten Tomatoes](https://www.rottentomatoes.com/), praising its emotional core and visual wit. Audiences were even more enthusiastic, granting it an 'A' grade from CinemaScore. This combination of critical and crowd support is driving its impressive staying power.

## Is Supergirl a Box Office Flop?

It’s too early to call it a flop, but the opening is undeniably soft. The new DC Universe film, *Supergirl*, directed by Craig Gillespie and starring Milly Alcock, is looking at an opening weekend of roughly $40 million. For a major superhero tentpole with a significant production and marketing budget, that’s a concerning start. It suggests the new DCU is still struggling to capture the broad audience it needs, despite the critical success of its first chapter, *Superman*.

The contrast between the two films this weekend is stark. One is a beloved 30-year-old franchise showing remarkable resilience. The other is a fresh reboot of a classic character failing to generate immediate excitement. For Disney and Pixar, the *[Toy Story 5](/movies/box-office-update-toy-story-5-holds-strong-supergirl-opens-lower-than-/)* box office performance is a welcome return to form for a brand that has faced questions about theatrical viability. For Warner Bros. and DC Studios, the *Supergirl* numbers indicate the rebuild is still a work in progress.
